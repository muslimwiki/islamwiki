# Islam

**Islam**<ref>/ˈɪzlɑːm, ˈɪzlæm/ *IZ-la(h)m*; Arabic: ٱلْإِسْلَام‎, <small>romanized:</small> *al-Islām*, IPA: [alʔɪsˈlaːm], <abbr>lit.</abbr> 'submission [to the will of God]'</ref> is the final and complete way of life revealed by [[Allah]] (ﷻ) for all of humanity, and it is followed by over 1.9 billion people across the world who are known as **[[Muslims]]**.<ref>"[https://www.pewresearch.org/topic/religion/religious-demographics/pew-templeton-global-religious-futures-project/ Pew-Templeton Global Religious Futures Project - Research and data from Pew Research Center]". 21 December 2022. [https://web.archive.org/web/20230205195540/https://www.pewresearch.org/topic/religion/religious-demographics/pew-templeton-global-religious-futures-project/ Archived] from the original on 5 February 2023. Retrieved 27 November 2023.</ref> Islam is generally, in secularist society, considered as apart of the [[Abrahamic religions]], and is based on the [[Qur'an]].<ref>[https://mpeb.ie/for-teachers/glossary-of-islamic-terms/ Glossary of Islamic Terms], retrieved 17 February 2025</ref> The message of Islam was perfected in the 7th century CE through the final Messenger, **[[Prophet Muhammad ﷺ]]**,<ref>"[https://www.worldhistory.org/islam/ Islam]". Worldhistory.org. 25 November 2019. Retrieved 26 February 2025.</ref> who was sent as a mercy to the worlds (Qur'an 21:107). However, the roots of Islam are not confined to that time period—rather, Islam is the original religion that began with **[[Adam]] (عليه السلام)**, the first human and prophet.

{{Cquote|Indeed, the religion in the sight of Allah is Islam.|{{qref|3|19|b=yl|c=y|y=si}}}}

Prophet Muhammad ﷺ did not bring a new religion, but came to confirm and complete the message of all previous prophets, including **[[Noah]] (Nūḥ), [[Abraham]] (Ibrāhīm), [[Moses]] (Mūsā), and [[Jesus]] (ʿĪsā)**—peace be upon them all. These prophets preached the worship of **one God ([[Tawhid|Tawḥīd]])** and submission to His will, which is the essence of Islam. Allah states:

{{Cquote|Say, 'We believe in Allah and what has been revealed to us and what was revealed to Abraham, Ishmael, Isaac, Jacob, and the Descendants, and what was given to Moses and Jesus and what was given to the prophets from their Lord. We make no distinction between any of them, and to Him we submit'|{{qref|2|136|b=yl|c=y|y=si}}}}

Although [[Judaism]] and [[Christianity]] are often referred to as part of the **Abrahamic faiths**, Islam does not consider itself a new branch or offshoot. Instead, it is the **original, pure monotheistic faith** restored in its final form through the Qur'an and the [[Sunnah]]. Muslims believe that previous revelations were true in their original form but were altered over time, whereas the **Qur'an** remains preserved and unchanged since its revelation over 1,400 years ago (Qur'an 15:9).

Islam is more than just a religion—it is a comprehensive system of guidance covering all aspects of life, including spiritual, moral, social, legal, and economic matters. It invites humanity to worship Allah alone, follow the example of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, and prepare for the Hereafter.

## Etymology
{{Further information|[[Muslims#Etymology]], [[S-L-M]]}}

The word **Islam** (Arabic: الإسلام) comes from the [[triliteral]] root **س-ل-م** (sīn-lām-mīm), which carries the meanings of submission, surrender, peace, and wholeness. Linguistically, **Islam** means "submission" or "surrender," referring specifically to submission to the will of Allah, the One and Only God. This submission is not out of compulsion or humiliation, but rather a willing, conscious, and peaceful surrender rooted in love, trust, and obedience to Allah. From this same root comes the word **salām** (سلام), meaning "peace," showing that through submission to Allah, one attains true peace — inwardly, outwardly, and ultimately in the Hereafter.

## Acts of Worship (The Five Pillars)
{{Main|Five Pillars of Islam}}
{{Further information|[[Ibadah]]}}

Islam is based on five key practices, known as the **[[Five Pillars of Islam]]**, which guide a Muslim's daily life and spiritual growth.

### Declaration of Faith (Shahada)
{{Main|Shahada}}

The *[[shahadah]]* ([[Arabic]]: الشهادة)<ref>[[Seyed Hossein Nasr|Nasr, Seyed Hossein]] (2003). pp. 3, 39, 85, 270–272. The Heart of Islam: Enduring Values for Humanity.</ref> is the fundamental declaration of faith in Islam, affirming belief in the oneness of God and the prophethood of Muhammad. The full statement is:

{{Cquote|*ashhadu an lā ilāha illā Allāh wa ashhadu anna Muhammad rasūl Allāh* <br/>(أشهد أن لا إله إلا الله وأشهد أن محمداً رسول الله)|"I bear witness that there is no god but God, and I bear witness that Muhammad is the messenger of God."<ref>[[N. Mohammad|Mohammad, N.]] 1985. "The doctrine of jihad: An introduction." [[Journal of Law and Religion]] 3(2):381–97.</ref>}}

This simple yet profound testimony serves as the foundation of Islamic belief and practice. Anyone who wishes to embrace Islam formally does so by reciting the *shahadah* in the presence of witnesses.<ref>[[Husain Kasim|Kasim, Husain]]. "Islam". In Salamone (2004), pp. 195–197. [[Frank Salamone|Salamone, Frank]], ed. (2004). [https://archive.org/details/encyclopediaofre00sala Encyclopedia of Religious Rites, Rituals, and Festivals]. Routledge Encyclopedias of Religion and Society. Vol. 6 (1st ed.). [[Routledge]]. [[ISBN]] 978-0-415-94180-8. [[JSTOR]] [https://www.jstor.org/stable/j.ctt1jd94wq j.ctt1jd94wq].</ref>

### Prayer (Salah)
{{Main|Salah}}
{{Further information|[[Masjid]], [[Jumu'ah]], [[Qibla]]}}

[[File:Mohammad_Al-Amin_Mosque_during_2019_Lebanese_revolution.jpg|alt=Interior of a large mosque with ornate chandeliers and carpets.|thumb|The mosque during the 2019 Lebanese revolution]]

In Islam, prayer — known as *ṣalāh* or *ṣalāt* ([[Arabic]]: الصلاة) — is a central act of worship that fosters a direct connection between the believer and God. It consists of a series of physical and verbal movements called *[[rak'āt]]*, which involve standing, [[bowing]], and [[prostrating]] in humility before God. Muslims are obligated to perform five prayers each day at prescribed times, always facing the direction of the [[Kaaba]] in Mecca. These prayers are offered in Arabic and require a state of ritual purity, maintained through *[[wuḍū']]* (partial ablution) or, in specific cases, *[[ghusl]]* (full-body purification).<ref>Esposito (2002b), pp. 18, 19 — (2002b). What Everyone Needs to Know about Islam. [[Oxford University Press]]. [[ISBN]] 978-0-19-515713-0.</ref>

### Charity (Zakat)
{{Main|Zakat}}
{{Further information|[[Sadaqah]]}}

Zakat ([[Arabic language|Arabic]]: زكاة) is one of the fundamental pillars of Islam, a sacred duty placed upon those whom Allah has blessed with wealth. It requires Muslims to give a set portion — typically 2.5% of their accumulated savings each year,<ref>Ahmed, Medani, and Sebastian Gianci. "Zakat." p. 479 in Encyclopedia of Taxation and Tax Policy.</ref> to support those in need, including the poor, debtors, travelers in difficulty, and others eligible under Islamic guidelines.

### Fasting (Sawm)
{{Main|Sawm}}
{{See also|Fasting during Ramadan}}

In Islam, fasting (*ṣawm*, [[Arabic]]: صوم) is a deeply spiritual act of worship, in which we abstain from food, drink, [[smoking]], and other physical needs from dawn until sunset. The most honored time for fasting is during the blessed month of [[Ramadan]], when it becomes a duty upon every adult Muslim who is able.<ref>"[https://www.britannica.com/topic/Ramadan Ramadan]". www.britannica.com. [https://web.archive.org/web/20231009215438/https://www.britannica.com/topic/Ramadan Archived] from the original on 9 October 2023. Retrieved 16 August 2023.</ref>

### Pilgrimage (Hajj)
{{Main|Hajj|Umrah}}
{{See also|Holiest sites in Islam}}

The Hajj ([[Arabic]]: حج) is the sacred pilgrimage to the holy city of [[Mecca]], and it is an obligation upon every Muslim who has the physical and financial ability to perform it at least once in their lifetime, during the month of [[Dhul-Hijjah]]. The rites of Hajj beautifully commemorate the profound legacy of [[Prophet Ibrahim]] (Abraham, peace be upon him), his wife [[Hajar]], and their son [[Isma'il]].

## Articles of Faith (The Six Pillars)
{{Main|'Aqīdah|Īmān}}

The foundational beliefs of Islam, often referred to as the **Six Articles of Faith,** include:

### Oneness of God (Allah)
{{Main|Allah}}

The core belief in Islam is *[[tawḥīd]]* (Arabic: توحيد), the absolute oneness and unity of God. While commonly understood as strict [[monotheism]], [[Islamic mysticism]] (especially [[Sufism]]) also carries [[panentheistic]] elements, emphasizing God's presence in all things.<ref>"[https://www.britannica.com/topic/tawhid Tawhid]". [[Encyclopædia Britannica]]. [https://web.archive.org/web/20211107041300/https://www.britannica.com/topic/tawhid Archived] from the original on 7 November 2021. Retrieved 17 September 2021.</ref>

### Angels
{{Main|Angels in Islam}}

In Islam, angels (*malāʾika*, Arabic: ملك) are spiritual beings mentioned in both the Qur'an<ref>Burge, Stephen (2015). Angels in Islam: Jalal al-Din al-Suyuti's al-Haba'ik fi akhbar al-mala'ik. p. 23; London: Routledge. <nowiki>ISBN 978-1-136-50473-0</nowiki>.</ref> and hadith<ref>Burge, Stephen (2015). Angels in Islam: Jalal al-Din al-Suyuti's al-Haba'ik fi akhbar al-mala'ik. p. 79; London: Routledge. <nowiki>ISBN 978-1-136-50473-0</nowiki>.</ref>. They are created by God to worship Him and carry out specific duties, such as delivering divine revelations, recording human deeds, and taking souls at the time of death.

### Prophets and Messengers
{{Main|Prophets and Messengers in Islam}}

In Islam, prophets (*[[anbiyā']]*, [[Arabic]]: أنبياء) are human beings chosen by God to convey His guidance to humanity. Among them, some are also called "messengers" (*[[rusul]]*, [[Arabic]]: رسول‎)<ref>Esposito, John, ed. (2003). [[The Oxford Dictionary of Islam]]. p. 225. [[Oxford University Press]]. <nowiki>ISBN 978-0-19-512558-0</nowiki>.</ref> because they were entrusted with delivering new scriptures.

### Revealed Scriptures
{{Main|Revelation in Islam|Qur'an}}
{{See also|History of the Qur'an}}

The central sacred text of Islam is the [[Qur'an]], which Muslims believe was revealed to the Prophet Muhammad by God through the archangel [[Jibrīl]] (Gabriel) over a period spanning from 610 CE<ref>Buhl, F.; Welch, A.T. "Muhammad". In Encyclopaedia of Islam Online (n.d.). Bearman, P.J.; Bianquis, Th.; Bosworth, C.E.; van Donzel, E.; Heinrichs, W.P., eds. (n.d.). Encyclopaedia of Islam Online. Brill Academic Publishers. ISSN 1573-3912.</ref> until Muhammad's death in 632 CE.

### Day of Judgment
{{Main|Yawm al-Qiyāmah|Islamic eschatology}}

Belief in the *Day of Resurrection* (*Yawm al-Qiyāmah*, [[Arabic]]: يوم القيامة) is a central tenet of Islam. Muslims believe that while the timing of this day is known only to God, its arrival is certain and has been predetermined.

### Divine Decree (Predestination)
{{Main|Qadr}}

In Islam, the concept of divine predestination (*al-qadāʾ wa'l-qadar*, [[Arabic]]: القضاء والقدر) refers to the belief that all events—whether good or bad—occur according to God's will and decree.

## Demographics

## Misconceptions and Clarifications

Islam, like many other global religions, has been subject to widespread misconceptions—often fueled by misinformation, isolated political events, and the actions of a misguided few who do not represent the faith as taught by the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ.

> "Invite to the way of your Lord with wisdom and good instruction, and argue with them in a way that is best." (Surah An-Nahl 16:125)

### Islam and Peace

One of the most common misconceptions is that Islam promotes violence or intolerance. This is a gross distortion of the faith. The very word "Islam" comes from the root word **س ل م** (S-L-M), which encompasses meanings of peace, submission, and safety.
